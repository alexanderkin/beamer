\begin{frame}{HYPOTHESIS}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \alert{Hypothesis 1:}The abundance, species richness, and evenness of generalist birds will increase with additional high-tempearture days, while those of specialist birds will decrease with additional high-temperature days.
    \item \alert{Hypothesis 2:}Bird metrics (abundance, species richness, and species evenness) in the drier regions will respond more negatively to increase in the high-temperature days.
    \item \alert{Hypothesis 3:}The potential negative impact of an increase in the high-temperature days on bird abundance, species richness, and evenness will be mitigated in the long-run.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{OBJECTIVE??}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item
    To examine the effects of climate change on whole bird population and two subgroups of bird species (specialist, generalist) by using three different metrics of birds: the number of birds, number of bird species and Shannon-weaver index on U.S. continent from 1981 to 2015.
    \item
    To provide large-scale empirical evidence on whether and to what extent the impact of climate change will be offset in the long-term due to the adaptation and evolution of bird population.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{MODEL}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Panel approach, the standard is clear
    \pause
    \item Account for potential omitted variables
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Time-invariant stuff
      \item Time trending stuff: linear/nonlinear trends
    \end{itemize}
    \begin{equation}
      \begin{aligned}
        B_{i,t}=\alpha + \sum_{z=1}^{36} \beta_{FE, z}C_{i,t,z} + \gamma D_{i,t} + \mu_i + \phi_t + \epsilon_{i,t},\\ \textit{$i=1,...,N$ and $t=1,...,T$}
        \label{equ:e1}
      \end{aligned}
    \end{equation}
    \item Bad controls
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{MODEL}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Linear adaptation $\rightarrow$ Panel Approach
    \item Adaption at the end of the study period $\rightarrow$ overestimate by panel approach
    \item Eestimates of short-run responses to weather might not  bound estimates of longer run response to climate
    \begin{equation}
      \begin{aligned}
        \bar{B}_{i,t_b} - \bar{B}_{i,t_a} = \alpha + \beta_{LD}(\bar{C}_{i,t_b,t} - \bar{C}_{i,t_a,t}) + \gamma_i + \epsilon_i
        \label{equ:e2} \\
        \textit{Multiyear periods a and b, each spanning T years} \\
        \bar{B}_{i,t_b} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t \in b} B_{i,t} \\ \bar{B}_{i,t_a} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t \in a} B_{i,t}
      \end{aligned}
    \end{equation}
    \hyperlink{Long Difference}{\beamerbutton{here}}
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{MODEL}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Long differences approximation utilizes variation in longer run climate change
    \item Immune to time-invariant omitted Variables
    \item The relationship between climate and bird biodiversity is estimated from long-term changes in average conditions instead of short-run year-to-year variation.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section*{Summary}

\begin{frame}{Summary}

  % Keep the summary *very short*.
  \footnotesize
  \begin{itemize}
  \item
  Overall, for the whole bird group, my results showed climate change had a significant impact on biodiversity of bird species in routes that were distributed across the United States.
  \item
    Swapping a day in the 0-15 °C range for one above 25 °C increases the decline of bird abundance, species richness and evenness by approximately 0.2\%~0.5\%.
  \item
  The impact of additional extreme heat temperature days (>25 °C degree) on specialist birds is almost three times larger than the generalist birds.
  \item
  Effect in the western and northern region will be more negative on the bird abundance(-1.5\%), species richness(-0.6\%) and Shannon-weaver index(-0.6\%).
  \item
  The long-run adaptation has offset only 20\% of the negative short-term impact of extreme heat(>25 °C) on bird species richness. Birds has no more adaptation to extreme heat in the long run.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
